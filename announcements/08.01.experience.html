More Hands-on Experiences

So much of the work that I've seen so far has been really great. I know that many of you may be new to some of the technologies and concepts, but you are doing a great job applying the concepts and learning as you go.

Emerging and Evolving Technologies

We discussed at the start that one of the primary reasons for not using a printed textbook is that the technologies and platforms we are working with are constantly changing. This is the nature of technology in the 21st century. On-line and on-demand services are constantly changing. New features are added, existing features are modified, and some features are removed entirely. And interfaces are updated frequently!

The concepts apply, and the fundamentals are the same, but the details, interfaces, and even some of the terminology may change over time. One of the things that engineers struggle with all the time in the real world is navigating the changing landscape. Sometimes it's and changing interface with a familiar-- or new tool. Sometimes it's a changing library or API and an application or system that is dependent on it needs to be updated or modified to work with the new version.

Some of the details and application steps for these exercises also are changing-- obviously at any time and outside of our control. Usually, I will work through the exercises and demonstrations before sharing them with you, and sometimes I have made edits, or adjustments, but sometimes I intentionally do not make adjustments to the steps or details. I am not trying to make things more difficult, but my primary goal is for you to experience and understand the concepts of what the technology is capable of. What I really don't want is to have students click here, click here, click here, click here, hit submit and move on without understanding what they've actually accomplished. This technology is really cool, it's important to understand what you've done. How you got there will change over time, but the capabilities are the takeaway.

For some, that means it can get frustrating when things don't work as expected, or when the steps don't follow the instructions. For others, figuring out how to get there is part of the fun and the learning experience. I know that most in the science and engineering fields probably grew up taking things apart to see how they work. Reading books is great, but doing is better.

Collaborative Learning and Problem Solving

I've been especially encouraged by the collaborative discussions and efforts I've seen on Discord. My students often hear me say that I want you to be challenged and frustrated at times and working through those challenges is when the real learning happens. But let me be clear. I want you to be frustrated, but I don't want you to stay frustrated! How long is too long? That's a very personal question and it will absolutely vary from person to person and even from time to time. For some, it may be 15 minutes. For others, it may be an hour or more. If you are stuck and frustrated, how do you deal with it? For me, I shift gears and work on something else, or walk away for a bit, or ask for help. When I come back to the problem, I have a fresh perspective and often see things that I missed before.

Keep using the resources available to you. Ask questions on Discord. Collaborate with your classmates. Use the internet to search for answers. Try different things. The instructions and steps are very important. There are steps which are required and not to be skipped, but the path to complete them may change slightly over time.

Different Perspectives

In addition to discovering the capabilities and concepts of the technologies, I also very much enjoy reading about your experiences and even impressions of the technology. It's interesting to see how it through the eyes of someone who is new to it. I find it rewarding to see the "light bulb" moments. And trust me, as many opinions and first impressions as you have shared, my impressions and opinions are often not aligned with yours. I deliberately try to avoid sharing my opinions and impressions in your technology experiences, because I want you to have your own experiences and form your own opinions. It's also why I do make use of the discussion boards in Canvas. I genuinely want you to share your experiences and impressions with each other. I find it fascinating to read about your experiences and perspectives, even (and especially) when they are different from mine.

For example, I have been working with Azure for many years, and I have a very good understanding and comfort level and trust of the capabilities. I have used other cloud service providers, but some of my experiences with others have not always been positive, and I personally tend to steer clients and my own projects away from some of those providers. In contrast, sometimes I read about student experiences with AWS and GCP and Alibaba and others and it sparks my curiosity, and might even drive me to explore cost analysis and comparisons, but I don't have to agree with your impressions to still find them valuable and interesting.

I use cloud services when it makes sense, and having knowledge of the capabilities has opened so many opportunities to find more and more uses for them. It is my sincere hope that demonstrating these for yourselves will give you new ideas and allow you to explore more and more uses for your own projects and solutions.

 

Keep up the great work! I look forward to hearing more from you.
